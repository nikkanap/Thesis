I did a research-based review of your manuscript, and the core idea is strong. The thesis is coherent, the gap is meaningful, and your methodology is substantially aligned with the three papers you named: Riley & Collins for bootstrap-based stability assessment and multi-level instability, Lopez-Martinez et al. for repeated-run instability and ranking-oriented measures, and Zhang & Bose for individual-level reliability via MRIP. Riley & Collins explicitly recommend repeated model-building in multiple bootstrap samples, define four stability levels, and use measures such as mean absolute prediction error and instability plots; Lopez-Martinez et al. show that repeated training on the same data can change patient-level outcomes even when global metrics stay stable; and Zhang & Bose frame MRIP as an individual-prediction reliability measure. ([University of Birmingham][1])

My overall verdict: the project is promising, but the manuscript still has a few high-priority methodological and citation issues that you should fix before treating it as defense-ready. The most important ones are below.

The single biggest methodological issue is your use of LinearSVC together with probability-based instability and reliability metrics. In the manuscript, many of your planned measures require probabilities or risk scores interpreted probabilistically: MAPE on predicted probabilities, 95% stability intervals for probabilities, Brier score, and MRIP. But `LinearSVC` does not provide `predict_proba`; its documented output is `decision_function`, and the docs do not list `predict_proba` at all. Scikit-learn’s calibration docs explicitly point to `CalibratedClassifierCV` as the way to obtain calibrated probabilities, and it even notes that the default estimator is `LinearSVC`, with calibration based on `decision_function` when available. So as written, your methodology is internally inconsistent unless you either calibrate `LinearSVC`, replace it with a classifier that outputs probabilities, or clearly separate probability-based metrics from score-based metrics. ([Scikit-learn][2]) Your current methods section still presents LinearSVC alongside probability-based metrics without resolving that issue. 

Related to that, your stochastic-instability design is not equally valid for all four models unless you specify the exact implementation details. In scikit-learn, `LogisticRegression` uses `random_state` only for `sag`, `saga`, or `liblinear`, while the default solver is `lbfgs`; and for `LinearSVC`, `random_state` matters for the dual coordinate descent case, while when `dual=False` the implementation is documented as non-random. That means your “vary the seed and retrain” procedure could produce little or no stochastic variation for some model/solver settings unless you deliberately choose stochastic solvers/settings. Right now the manuscript says you will vary random seeds across all models, but it does not specify solver choices or which models are actually expected to exhibit seed-driven instability. ([Scikit-learn][3]) 

Your methodology is conceptually aligned with Riley, Lopez-Martinez, and Zhang, but it is still under-specified operationally. Riley & Collins do not just say “use bootstraps”; they explicitly discuss repeating the full model-building process over multiple bootstrap samples, often on the order of hundreds to a thousand, and then deriving instability plots, MAPE, and calibration/classification instability summaries. Your manuscript still says “a large number” of bootstrap samples and “multiple” random seeds without fixing those values. ([University of Birmingham][1])  Before defense, you should lock down at least these items in writing: number of bootstrap samples, number of seeds, whether K in Top-K Jaccard is 10% or something else, whether MRIP uses a fixed tolerance or model-specific tolerance, which hyperparameters are held fixed, how class labels are thresholded, and whether any probability calibration is done for non-probabilistic models.

Your literature review is much better than the older version you showed earlier, but it still has a few citation overstatements. The clearest one is the recurring claim that COMPAS used regression modeling or that logistic regression was used in the development of both COMPAS and MnSTARR. For MnSTARR, that is supported: the OJP abstract explicitly says logistic regression modeling was used, with bootstrap resampling for predictor refinement and internal validation. ([Office of Justice Programs][4]) But for COMPAS, the Practitioner’s Guide supports only that COMPAS is a statistically based assessment system developed empirically; it does not cleanly establish a specific logistic-regression architecture in the way your manuscript phrases it.  Yet your manuscript still says on page 3 that COMPAS and MnSTARR “use regression modeling,” and on page 4 that logistic regression “was used in the development of both the MnSTARR and COMPAS models.”  I would change COMPAS wording to something like “statistically based” or “empirically developed risk-assessment tool,” while keeping the logistic-regression wording for MnSTARR only.

A second literature-review issue is the sentence saying subsequent works “[24, 26, 27] further reinforced that no single model dominates across all evaluation metrics, highlighting the importance of data quality and feature representation.” Your “no single model dominates” point is directionally reasonable, especially given Duwe & Kim and metric-dependent results in comparative work. Tollenaar & van der Heijden report that classical methods do equally well as or better than modern counterparts, and Duwe & Kim report modest gaps with no method best in all 10 scenarios. ([OUP Academic][5]) But the “data quality and feature representation” part is not tightly supported by those specific citations. That part is better attributed to the systematic-review literature rather than to all those model-comparison papers. ([MDPI][6])

Your problem statement and objectives are solid, but the wording occasionally becomes too absolute. For example, “none have explored their instability in the same domain” is strong unless you are very sure the literature is exhaustive. A safer phrase is “comparatively little attention has been given” or “to our knowledge, prior work has not directly examined…” That would still preserve your gap while sounding more defensible. The same applies to the reliability claim: saying stability and reliability are “directly linked” is fine in a broad sense, but they are not identical constructs in your own framework, because you also separately import MRIP from Zhang. So your writing should make the relationship hierarchical: instability threatens reliability, rather than stability simply equaling reliability. ([University of Birmingham][1]) 

The metrics section is one of the strongest parts of the manuscript, but it needs cleanup. Your use of MAPE, stability intervals, Top-K Jaccard, CII, PR-AUC variability, Brier, and MRIP gives you a legitimately multidimensional evaluation, and that matches your stated goal of going beyond aggregate performance. ([University of Birmingham][1])  But there are still two practical issues. First, you still have missing citations marked as `[?]` for Brier Score and SHAP.  Second, if you keep Brier, describe it carefully: recent methodological work notes that Brier is widely used for binary probabilistic predictions, but its interpretation depends on event prevalence and it should not be overclaimed as a direct measure of clinical utility. ([PMC][7]) So Brier is fine here, but phrase it as a probability accuracy / calibration-oriented metric, not as a standalone reliability proof.

Your SHAP plan is reasonable as an exploratory extension, but you should frame it carefully. Right now the manuscript says SHAP will identify which features “contribute to instability.” That is a bit too strong. What SHAP can show more safely is which features have important and/or variable contributions across repeated runs; that makes them candidates associated with instability, not definitive causal drivers of instability. This matters especially because feature-attribution stability is itself a methodological issue. The idea is still good; just soften the wording. 

There are also several manuscript-quality issues you should clean up immediately because they make the draft look unfinished even though the research idea is good. The PDF still contains a placeholder section title, “Initial Experiment and Results (later pa adi if may results na lel),” plus stray text on page 12 (“importing the dataset from huggingface, training the programs,”), plus unresolved `[?]` citations. There are also small language issues such as “a individual,” “redivist,” “other say,” and some awkward heading transitions.  These are easy fixes, but they materially affect how polished the manuscript feels.

If I were prioritizing revisions, I would do them in this order. First, fix the LinearSVC probability/reliability inconsistency. Second, make the experimental design fully explicit: exact bootstrap count, seed count, thresholds, hyperparameters, and calibration plan. Third, correct the citation overclaims around COMPAS and the model-comparison literature. Fourth, clean up the unfinished draft artifacts and missing references. After that, the manuscript will read much more like a defensible thesis proposal rather than a strong draft with a few structural gaps.

So the short verdict is: yes, your methodology is genuinely anchored in Lopez-Martinez, Riley & Collins, and Zhang, and that is the strongest part of the thesis. But before submission/defense, you need to tighten the implementation logic and clean up a few citation claims, especially around COMPAS and LinearSVC. ([University of Birmingham][1])
